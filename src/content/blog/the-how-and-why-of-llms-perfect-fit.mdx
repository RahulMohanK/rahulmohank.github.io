## Impacts on developers

Developers are forced to become ideators who wears the cap of a product owner, who identifies the requirements of the business and fills the gap between a user and a software developer. 
Problem solving is moreover about defining the spec (spec-kit, skills.md) and agents does the implementation. Developers job is to chisel and make the product perfect with the prompts,
while agents does the heavy lifting.

There are advantages and disadvantages to this approach. Lets be optimistic and start with the advantages.

- Agent based software engineering defies the traditional seniority based heirarchies in large organizations. Every developer, irrspective of their number of years of experience in 
the field, can sit in the driving seat. They can envision the end-to-end of a product with lesser focus on the underlying implementation as this is ensured by agentic tools already.
But this does not deny the fact that a senior engineer with first hand experience in buiding and delivering software, has upper hand in the business domain.
- In most cases agentic development guarentees a working solution with commentable end user satisfaction and performance.
- It drastically increases the pace of development and delivery cycle. Things which took weeks before, now only takes days in worst case, to reach the customers.
- It provides newer ways of learning things, sometimes the solutions might be much better that what we thought in thw first place.

Talking about the disadvantages, software developemt gets into its peak when we implement something which was ideated. Implementing it ourselves always reaffirms and validates the 
architectural desicions we arrived at, while designing the solution. Now the implementation part can be totally handed over to agents;coding and debugging feedback loop is something that developers
doesn't concern anymore. This is good and bad at the same time. Humans are as good as they repeat an activity. Developers who moves away from the coding and debugging part can loose their traction
to read and review the code written by llms. This a very concerning problem. As far as human is in the loop of software developmwnt lifecycle, if human is to takeover and solve a critical production
bug that agents couldnt solve, then developer should be aware of what the code base does. Developers who have drifted away from programming can face backlashes when such an event occur.
Otherwise a developer should never write software at all, instead they should just connect the dots between the modules built by agents to get the overall architecture working. Looking at 
the pace at which ai tools progress, there is a high chance that we dont even need to see the code anymore, agents itself will provide us the final product we desired.
([Bun rewrite to Rust](https://github.com/oven-sh/bun/pull/30412), [Claudes C compiler](https://github.com/anthropics/claudes-c-compiler)).

On a broader aspect, what does it mean by using an external system as our second brain ? Lets take a real world example; we consume news from multiple social platforms, we already know that not all
of them will be true - but still we tend to believe what is false to be true sometimes. How does is happen ? If 7 out of 10 news articles displayed in a platform are correct and validated by the public
then we automatically tend to believe the the rest 3 - A [halo effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halo_effect) will be subconsciously created within our mind. The same effect happens when we use GPT's 
to offload our tasks and this is true for everybody who uses these tools. We will scrutinize the results from llms rigorously at the begining and towards completion, we will accept whatever it provides -
we simply cannot match the pace at which results are generated. Things get even worse when multiple agents are used in parallel.
We also tend to believe that the solution is built by us - generally that is true
because we initated the developemt with a prompt,but in reality the ownership is held by the tool itself - it creates an illusion of productivity. We would be aware of the overall structure of the solution
 but all the nitty-gritty details would be taken for granted.



## Impacts on commoditizing software 

For decades the industry have been monetizing software based on api usage of its product which costs them the compute, memory and storage usage in a managed cloud or on-prem,
on top of which the solution is mounted. In agentic tools era, token is the new oil and commodity. Earlier if it was cost for running cloud and managing developers that companies 
had to manage, now they have an additional cost of token usage. Its hard not use agents to build solutions, as engineers are more and more accustomed to writing prompt for research 
and solutiong, as it is faster, more efficient when compared to googling. Companies on other end will be compelled to subscribe to any LLM vendor for various reasons including market
competition, speed, product exposure and mainly security issues, posed when developers use free GPT's available. 

There are only few deep tech companies who offer GPT subscriptions and they control how token is spent. Organizations who consume these agentic tools doesn't have the fine grained
control to observe where exactly token is expended and only option that is left with them is to cap the usage per user. Therefore the first uncertainity is that introduction of each 
model in the market would be priced more that its predecessor.

Token expenditure should become proportional to the speed of delivery and quality of software - more productivity, more PR's, more deliverables, but in reality for a SaaS company its 
hard to evaluate token usage with respect to useful features made available to the customers (uber AI budget). The latter would be the second uncertainty.

I believe that it would be harder to position fee for a product in the market with such fluctuating parameters.


## Conclusion



It is true that every new model introduced are more capable that their predecessors, but how do we evaluate that the current problem we have even require a 
bigger, costlier model. We expect that the bi`gger and newer models solves whatever problems we have despite knowing the complexity of the problem in hand
Most of the time very small and less costlier model is required for solving the problem. This is something that companies cashes us - They sell a model for its
higher rating in benchmarks but in reality we dont even know whether we need that model to solve our problem.

Does every company run LLM's in their own infra in future and they have a dedicated opsa team to manage it ? Do evey company have a research team who are
not mere users of LLM's but are capable of architecting LLM's for the need of the company ?
